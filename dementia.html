Causes of Memory Loss and Dementia in Older Adults

Description: Plaques and tangles disrupt brain cell function, leading to cognitive decline.

Key Findings: Well-documented in research on amyloid plaques and tau proteins.

Sources: National Institute on Aging, Alzheimer’s Association

Description: Caused by reduced blood flow to the brain, often from strokes or vascular conditions.

Key Findings: Stroke-related damage can lead to progressive cognitive decline.

Sources: Stroke, The Lancet Neurology

Description: Abnormal protein deposits (Lewy bodies) in the brain disrupt memory and motor control.

Key Findings: Cognitive decline linked to Lewy bodies is well-researched.

Sources: Brain, Nature Reviews Neurology

Description: Affects frontal and temporal lobes, impacting behavior and memory.

Key Findings: Research shows genetic mutations and protein buildup as causes.

Sources: Nature Reviews Neurology, Brain

Description: Neurodegenerative disorder that also leads to cognitive decline in later stages.

Key Findings: Cognitive impairment common in Parkinson’s patients.

Sources: Movement Disorder Society, Parkinson’s Disease Foundation

Description: Repeated head trauma leads to brain degeneration and cognitive decline.

Key Findings: Strong evidence from studies on athletes (e.g., football players).

Sources: JAMA Neurology, The New England Journal of Medicine

Description: Prolonged alcohol abuse leads to memory issues, including Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome.

Key Findings: Documented link between heavy alcohol use and brain damage.

Sources: Alcoholism: Clinical & Experimental Research

Description: Certain infections can affect the brain and lead to cognitive decline.

Key Findings: Research shows HIV and untreated syphilis can cause dementia.

Sources: The Lancet, Journal of Neurovirology

Description: Some drugs, especially sedatives, can impair memory and cognition.

Key Findings: Certain drug classes (e.g., benzodiazepines) linked to memory issues.

Sources: The British Journal of Clinical Pharmacology, JAMA Internal Medicine

Description: Deficiency in vitamins like B12 and thiamine can impair brain function.

Key Findings: Research confirms B12 and other deficiencies contribute to cognitive decline.

Sources: The American Journal of Clinical Nutrition

Description: Poor sleep or lack of oxygen due to apnea affects cognitive function over time.

Key Findings: Sleep apnea linked to memory loss and dementia risk.

Sources: Neurology, Sleep Medicine Reviews

Description: Conditions like diabetes and high blood pressure increase the risk of dementia.

Key Findings: Strong correlation between chronic diseases and cognitive decline.

Sources: Journal of Alzheimer’s Disease, Diabetes Care

Description: Mental health issues can exacerbate memory problems or mimic dementia.

Key Findings: Depression can cause pseudo-dementia, leading to memory loss.

Sources: The American Journal of Geriatric Psychiatry

Description: Low levels of thyroid hormones affect brain function.

Key Findings: Research shows hypothyroidism is associated with memory loss.

Sources: Endocrine Reviews, The Journal of Clinical Endocrinology & Metabolism

Description: Dehydration or imbalances in electrolytes can cause confusion and memory issues.

Key Findings: Dehydration is a common cause of confusion in older adults.

Sources: The Journal of Gerontology, The American Journal of Kidney Diseases

Description: Tumors in cognitive regions can lead to memory and cognitive problems.

Key Findings: Research shows brain tumors affecting memory and cognition.

Sources: Neurosurgery, Neuro-Oncology

Description: Exposure to environmental toxins can impair cognitive function.

Key Findings: Studies link toxic exposure to increased dementia risk.

Sources: Environmental Health Perspectives, The Journal of Toxicology
